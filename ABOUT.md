# Who is Naboraj Sarkar?

This page is not a resume. It is the story behind the code. It is the life behind the labs, the late nights, the first breakthroughs, and the profound reasons I continue to engineer systems in public.

## Who I Am

I am a software engineer, open-source maintainer, and lifelong learner driven by an endless curiosity to understand how systems work. I did not start coding to build a career—I started because I could not stop fixing things that felt broken. In a small room with a low-end laptop and a slow connection, I discovered the quiet victory of making something function when nobody else believed it could. Today, I am the maintainer of active open-source ecosystems and an engineer focused on intelligent software, developer tools, and automation.

## What Drives Me

The world is full of fragmented systems, repetitive tasks, and unnecessary friction. What drives me is the challenge of architecting elegant solutions to complex problems. 
- **Resilience:** When something breaks, I do not see defeat. I see the next iteration. Every bug is an opportunity to harden the architecture.
- **Impact:** I build because I want technology to be useful, accessible, and meaningful. A system only matters if it solves a real human problem.
- **Honesty:** I do not hide messy work. I share what I build, what I try, and what fails. That honesty makes the lessons more real and the progress more valuable.

## How I Think

I believe in **systems over shortcuts**. 
At first, I focused on what worked in the moment. Then, I learned the power of thinking in complete systems. I began to design how data flows, how tools connect, and how an entire workflow can be simplified for the next developer. 

Instead of shipping the first idea that works, I question my underlying assumptions. I spend deliberate time studying physics, mathematics, and critical thinking. This sharper reasoning connects directly to how I engineer—whether I am directing AI agents to orchestrate workflows or building cybersecurity defenses to ensure software holds up under extreme pressure.

## How I Learn

My journey is not a straight line. It is a continuous loop of hypothesis, experimentation, and honest feedback.
- **Learning in Public:** Pushing code to a public repository taught me how to learn from others. It exposed my blind spots and corrected my assumptions. Building in public became my deliberate choice and my greatest strength.
- **Experimentation:** The first time I watched a sensor send live values to a dashboard, it changed my perspective. It taught me that software and physical hardware belong to the exact same story. 

## How I Engineer

It is one thing to build an isolated script. It is another entirely to run and scale a living open-source repository. 
My engineering focus has evolved from building solo prototypes to actively maintaining projects like **Birthday Bloom** and **Ghost Engine v5**. This means handling live contributor activity, designing robust CI/CD pipelines, automating PR labeling, and writing crystal-clear documentation. I build for scale, maintainability, and the developer experience.

## What I Have Built

The first meaningful project I built was born out of pure necessity. It was not polished, but it solved a real problem I faced every day. The first time a user said, *"This helped me,"* I finally understood why engineering matters. It is a bridge between a problem and a real person. Today, my systems reach further, integrating modern LLM workflows, full-stack applications, and robust automation pipelines.

## Where I'm Going

The hardest lesson I have learned is to slow down and build systems that last. Quick fixes are easy; meaningful architecture is not. Moving forward, my continuous engineering journey is focused on:
- Designing autonomous, multi-agent AI systems.
- Architecting high-performance developer tooling.
- Exploring the intersection of embedded hardware and cloud infrastructure.
